Does Qeynos really have the densest quest connectivity?

Reconstructing a per-zone content-connectivity graph from the EverQuest world database — NPCs linked by shared factions and shared quest items — to test a friend's claim with actual graph metrics.

📄 Research paper — Making Machine-Drawn Ontologies Look Hand-Drawn · lit review & proposal →

VerdictMostly yes — as a city, and if you value diversity of connections

✔ Hypothesis supported (with nuance)

Treating “the city of Qeynos” as its natural cluster (South + North + Aqueducts), Qeynos ranks #1 of every city in EQ for quest-web richness — a blend of quest-giver count, how densely those quest-givers interlink, and faction diversity. It roughly doubles its nearest rival, Freeport.

The nuance: no single Qeynos zone tops any single metric. Shadow Haven has more quest NPCs but only 6 factions (a neutral Luclin bazaar); Thurgadin is denser but a one-faction dwarf hold; dragon dungeons like Skyshrine win naive graph density by being a single monolithic clique. Qeynos wins precisely because it is broad and varied — 67 distinct factions woven through 160 quest-givers.

So your friend is right on the strongest reading, but “densest” depends on the definition: Qeynos loses raw density, and wins richness & diversity.

3Ontology view — the Qeynos knowledge graph

The graphs above are undirected co-membership (“these two NPCs share a faction”). Here it’s a proper ontology: a directed, typed graph where every edge is an action phrase — subject —predicate→ object triples. Six entity types and fifteen relations — including derived faction alliances inferred from how NPCs co-adjust standings, and has dialog mentioning, extracted by reading all ~293 Qeynos NPCs' quest-script dialogue with an LLM (catching references by name and by epithet/role, e.g. “the governor” → Antonius Bayle) and linking each to a canonical entity.

5How this was built

EQ “quests” are Perl scripts kept in a separate repo — they aren’t in the database. But the ontology quests run on is: which NPCs belong to which factions, and which items pass between them. So this reconstructs each zone as a graph:

  • Nodes — every distinct NPC that spawns in the zone (via spawn2 → spawnentry → npc_types).
  • Faction ties — two NPCs are linked if they share a faction in their faction hit-list (npc_faction_entries), weighted by 1 / faction size so sharing a tight allegiance (real relationship) counts far more than sharing a universal one.
  • Item ties — two NPCs linked if they share an item (loot or merchant) held by ≤ 40 NPCs globally, weighted by 1 / item frequency. This drops commodities (diamonds, spell runes, fine-steel) and keeps quest-relevant items.
  • Metrics — density, weighted degree, clustering, connected components, greedy-modularity communities, plus a quest-giver-only subgraph (npc_types.isquest).

Why rarity-weighting matters: without it, a dragon dungeon where 194 dragons all share one faction becomes a near-complete graph (density 0.94) and “wins”. That’s faction monotony, not quest richness. Weighting by rarity, and rewarding faction diversity, makes “dense connectivity” mean tightly & meaningfully interlinked.

Caveats: NPC turn-in relationships that live only in quest scripts aren’t captured; “isquest” is the DB’s own flag and is imperfect; city clusters are hand-grouped. The ranking is robust to reasonable variations of these choices.
